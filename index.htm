ON ONE SIDE:

“DISEMBOWELING OUR MONUMENTS, or The Carved Ones.”

· Monuments with a capital "M"

In a way, stone is one of the only materials that has served as medium for the whole of our history. Stone inscriptions are the traces of a history past. They are the mark that ancestral civilizations have left in the space we inhabit for the time being, and will inevitably leave marked with our ruins for the future.

All is fossils.

SENATVS·POPVLVS·QVE·ROMANVS IMP·CAESARI·DIVI·NERVAE·F·NERVAE TRAIANO·AVG·GERM·DACICO·PONTIF MAXIMO·TRIB·POT·XVII·IMP·VI·COS·VI·P·P AD·DECLARANDVM·QVANTAE·ALTITVDINIS MONS·ET·LOCVS·TANT[IS·OPER][b]IBVS·SIT·EGESTVS2

This is a transcription of the Trajan column, built after the Roman emperor Trajan wiped a whole nation in the Dacian wars. You might not be able to decipher this inscription, but it does seem that its content holds some intrinsic importance, even some power.3 It could be said that they are the fossils of our past—inlaid in those pieces centuries of history that stand and withstand time, war, and paradigm shifts galore. Differently from fossils though, the marks we leave are not the product of chance, rather a deliberate act of forced remembrance. These inscriptions are the names and stories of the people that were chosen to be seen as our history—and somebody had to be the one to choose.

Monuments hold the subtle power of installing themselves in our subconscious, being evepresent in the spaces we occupy, pass, and rest by. In their solemn light—insidiously erected—they might as well be the stories of conquerors and heroes. Celebratory of something that we may not even know what it is, but celebrate it as if they were our deeds documented there.

Monuments then become agents of memory and remembrance; they serve as evidence of something that we would like to remember and carry onto our future and teach our children. And they can be a number of different things: an oral story, a book, a vase, a poster; it all hinges on how we relate to it, anchoring our memories on that specific object.

But a monument is not simply a memento4; it must be something that ties a group of people to a collective memory. It’s much more potent than just an object that reminds you of something or someone, it’s a vessel through which a group can decide to canonize a person or event—categorize it as relevant for our collective evolution. Thus, Monuments become signifiers of our history.

But in the context of a lot of our society’s Monuments, who are the people being commemorated? We see figures being deemed historic without any context to their heroicism. And in comes the banality of this evil: these monuments are erected by people that, maybe, genuinely thought their heroes were worthy of that status, and all others thought nothing of it, or didn’t have the place to protest. It all becomes dangerous when they are mindlessly taken as the truth, when they go unnoticed or unquestioned. We must retroactively look at all our Monuments, and see them in a new, more critical, light—be vigilant...
